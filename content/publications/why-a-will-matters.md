## The Reality of Dying Without a Will

In Jamaica, if you die without a valid will — known as dying *intestate* — your estate is distributed according to the rules set out in the Intestates' Estates and Property Charges Act. These rules may not reflect your wishes.

This means that the people you intended to provide for may receive less than you planned, or nothing at all. Family members may also find themselves in prolonged and expensive legal disputes over your estate.

## What a Will Does

A properly drafted will allows you to:

- **Name your beneficiaries** — specify exactly who receives what from your estate
- **Appoint an executor** — the person responsible for carrying out your wishes
- **Protect minor children** — name a guardian and establish provisions for their care
- **Avoid family disputes** — leave clear instructions that reduce the potential for disagreement
- **Include specific bequests** — specify who inherits particular items of sentimental or financial value

## Who Needs a Will?

Simply put: anyone who owns property, has dependants, or wants control over what happens to their assets should have a will. This includes:

- Homeowners and landowners
- Parents of minor children
- Business owners
- Anyone with savings, investments, or personal property of value
- Persons in non-married relationships who wish to provide for a partner

## Requirements for a Valid Will in Jamaica

Under the Wills Act, a valid will must be:

1. In writing
2. Signed by the testator (the person making the will)
3. Witnessed by two persons present at the same time, who also sign the will

Witnesses should not be beneficiaries of the will, as this can invalidate their bequest.

## Keeping Your Will Up to Date

Life changes — and your will should reflect those changes. You should review and update your will when:

- You marry or divorce
- You have children or grandchildren
- You acquire or dispose of significant property
- A beneficiary or executor named in your will passes away
