## Introduction

Being a victim of crime is a traumatic experience. Beyond the immediate impact, many people face uncertainty about what happens next within the justice system — and whether their voice will be heard.

Jamaican law provides a number of protections and rights for crime victims. Understanding these rights is the first step toward asserting them.

## The Right to Report

Every person has the right to report a crime to the Jamaica Constabulary Force (JCF). You may make a report at any police station. If the police decline to take your report without good reason, you can seek assistance from the Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) or obtain private legal advice.

## The Right to Legal Representation

As a victim, you have the right to retain your own attorney to:

- Advise you on your legal position
- Represent your interests in civil proceedings arising from the criminal matter
- Guide you through giving a witness statement
- Advise you on compensation claims

This is separate from the State's prosecution of the accused.

## Witness Protection

If you are a witness to a serious crime and fear reprisal, Jamaica's Witness Protection Act may apply. The programme provides safety measures and support to ensure you can give evidence without endangering yourself or your family.

Contact the Witness Protection Unit through your attorney or the DPP's office.

## Compensation

Jamaica's Criminal Injuries (Compensation) Act allows victims of violent crime to apply for financial compensation from the State in certain circumstances. Applications are made to the Criminal Injuries Compensation Board.

Your attorney can advise whether you qualify and assist with the application process.

## Your Role in the Criminal Trial

As a victim, you may be called as a witness in the criminal trial of the accused. Your attorney can:

- Explain the trial process to you
- Help you understand what will be expected of you in court
- Advise you on what to do if you receive communications from the accused or their associates

## If the Accused Is Acquitted

An acquittal in the criminal court does not prevent you from pursuing civil remedies. In many cases, a civil claim for damages can succeed even where the criminal prosecution did not.
